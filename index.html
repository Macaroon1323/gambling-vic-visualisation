Gambling in Victoria: Regional Trends and Impacts (2011–2025)

This visualisation explores electronic gaming machine (EGM) gambling trends across Victoria, examining local government area (LGA) expenditure, density, and social impact. Data is sourced from the Victorian Gambling and Casino Control Commission (VGCCC) and associated public health datasets. Each chart contributes to understanding the scale and distribution of gambling behaviour across regions and over time.

The map illustrates total player losses per adult across Victorian LGAs in 2024. Regions with higher spending are concentrated around metropolitan and inner-suburban areas, with notable peaks in municipalities such as Brimbank, Casey, and Greater Dandenong. Regional areas generally record lower expenditure per capita, though some exceptions exist where venue density is higher.

The scatter plot allows comparison between gambling expenditure and machine density per 1,000 adults across LGAs. Each point represents a local area, sized by venue count. The interactive year slider highlights changes in gambling distribution from 2011 to 2021, showing consistent high density in urban areas but gradual increases in some regional towns.

Long-Term Expenditure Trends

Gambling expenditure across Victoria has fluctuated over the last decade. Despite brief declines during pandemic-related closures, the overall trend has risen steadily from 2011 to 2021. This sustained growth highlights how EGM gambling remains deeply embedded in local entertainment economies.

Top 10 LGAs by Player Loss (Sept 2025)

Recent figures show that gambling losses remain heavily concentrated within Melbourne’s outer suburban regions. LGAs such as Casey, Hume, Brimbank, and Greater Dandenong consistently rank among the state’s highest, driven by large venue numbers and population size. This chart presents the ten LGAs with the greatest player losses recorded in September 2025.

Alcohol and Gambling Correlation

When gambling losses are compared with alcohol-related hospitalisations, similar upward trends emerge. Both metrics suggest an increasing burden of addictive behaviours over the last decade. The relationship between these two indicators may reflect broader social and economic pressures influencing risk-taking behaviour in Victoria.
